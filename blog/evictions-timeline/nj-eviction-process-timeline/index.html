NJ Eviction Process Timeline: Step-by-Step Guide for Landlords (2026)

By Proactive Property Management · Published July 2026
By Proactive Property Management · July 29, 2026 · Proactive Property Management

Legal timeframes cited below are based on NJSA 2A:18-53 et seq. and standard Special Civil Part practice. All specific notice periods and court deadlines carry attorney-review-pending status — confirm with qualified NJ counsel before acting. This page describes the process and PPM's service; it is not legal advice.

Two Starting Points: Non-Payment vs. Lease Violation

Every NJ eviction begins with a qualifying ground. The two most common grounds for residential landlords are:

  • Non-payment of rent — tenant has failed to pay rent when due (NJSA 2A:18-53(b)). This track uses a shorter notice period and moves faster.
  • Lease violation (non-payment of other obligations, disorderly conduct, willful damage, etc.) — a different statutory ground under NJSA 2A:18-53(c) and related subsections, generally requiring a longer notice period and a documented cure opportunity.

Choosing the wrong ground — or mixing them in the same notice — is the single most common error that forces landlords to start over. Getting the trigger right on day one is what compresses the overall timeline.

Step 1 — Notice to Quit

Before any court filing, the landlord must serve a legally compliant Notice to Quit on the tenant. The notice requirements differ by ground:

  • Non-payment of rent: A 3-day Notice to Quit is generally required under NJSA 2A:18-53(b) (attorney-review-pending). The notice must state the amount owed and demand payment or vacatur.
  • Lease violation / month-to-month tenancy termination: A 30-day Notice to Quit is generally required (attorney-review-pending). Specific violations — disorderly conduct, damage, certain criminal activity — have their own notice specifications under NJSA 2A:18-53(c) through (m).

How PPM handles this step: We prepare the correct notice form, verify the delivery method (personal service or certified mail), and document proof of service — the foundation of the entire court record.

Step 2 — Complaint Filed at Special Civil Part

Once the notice period expires without cure or vacatur, the landlord files a Landlord/Tenant Complaint at the Special Civil Part of the Superior Court in the county where the property is located. The complaint must specify the statutory ground and attach the notice.

  • Filing fees apply (county-set; attorney-review-pending on current amounts).
  • The complaint must match the notice ground exactly — any mismatch is grounds for dismissal.
  • Only the named property owner (or a licensed property manager acting under a management agreement) may file on behalf of the property.

How PPM handles this step: We maintain a court-runner relationship with each county's Special Civil Part, verify complaint accuracy against the original notice, and file same-day once the notice period clears.

Step 3 — Summons Served, Hearing Scheduled

After filing, the court issues a summons and schedules a hearing — typically within approximately 5–6 weeks of the filing date (attorney-review-pending; actual scheduling varies by county and docket load). The summons is served on the tenant by a court officer or constable.

  • The hearing date is set by the court; landlords cannot request an earlier slot.
  • If service of the summons fails, the landlord must request re-service, which extends the timeline.
  • Some counties are faster (Essex: often 3–4 weeks); others run longer (Hudson, Bergen: 6–8 weeks in high-volume periods) — attorney-review-pending on current averages.

How PPM handles this step: We calendar the hearing date immediately, prepare the landlord's documentary evidence package (ledger, lease, all notices with proof of service), and confirm court officer service before the hearing.

Step 4 — LT Court Hearing

At the Landlord/Tenant hearing, the judge hears both sides. Common outcomes:

  • Judgment for possession — landlord prevails; a warrant for removal may be requested.
  • Consent order / payment plan — tenant agrees to pay arrears by a deadline; if they default, landlord can request a warrant without a new hearing.
  • Dismissal — notice was defective, ground not proven, or tenant cured arrears before the hearing. Landlord must re-notice if grounds still exist.
  • Adjournment — judge grants tenant more time; adds days or weeks to the timeline.

New Jersey LT hearings move quickly — typically 5–15 minutes per case. A clean record wins; a messy one loses on procedure even when the facts favor the landlord.

How PPM handles this step: We attend the hearing with the full file, present the ledger and notice history in the format judges expect, and coordinate with the owner's attorney if legal representation is required or advisable.

Step 5 — Warrant for Removal

After a judgment for possession, the landlord requests a Warrant for Removal from the court. The warrant is not issued immediately — under NJSA 2A:42-10.16 and related rules, there is a mandatory waiting period after judgment during which the tenant may pay all arrears and stay the warrant (attorney-review-pending on exact current periods).

  • For non-payment cases, the tenant historically has had the right to "pay and stay" up to a defined number of times per year — this right has been subject to legislative amendment; confirm current rules with counsel.
  • Once issued, the warrant is delivered to the Special Civil Part officer for execution.

How PPM handles this step: We track the warrant request, confirm issuance, and coordinate the lockout date with the officer — avoiding the common delay of an owner not knowing when the officer is actually available.

Step 6 — Officer Lockout

The final step is physical execution of the warrant by a court-authorized officer (typically a Special Civil Part Officer or, in some counties, a constable). The officer posts a final notice on the unit giving the tenant a short period — typically 3 business days (attorney-review-pending) — to vacate voluntarily. If the tenant has not vacated by that date, the officer returns to supervise the lockout.

  • The landlord or property manager must be present at the lockout with a locksmith.
  • Any belongings left behind are subject to New Jersey's abandoned property rules — do not remove or discard without following proper procedure (attorney-review-pending on specific requirements).
  • The unit may be re-keyed and re-marketed immediately after the officer clears it.

How PPM handles this step: We schedule the locksmith, coordinate attendance with the officer, document the unit condition at entry, and trigger our make-ready checklist so the unit gets back to market as quickly as possible.

How a Property Manager Compresses the Timeline

The NJ eviction process has fixed court-controlled windows that no one can skip. What is controllable — and where landlords lose weeks — is everything on the landlord's side of the file:

  • Documentation from day one — a certified mail log, timestamped photos, a clean payment ledger, and a signed lease with the right clauses are the difference between a hearing that lasts 8 minutes and one that gets continued.
  • Notice accuracy — wrong notice period or wrong statutory ground = dismissal + restart. PPM prepares notices from a template library reviewed for current NJ law.
  • Court-runner relationships — filing same-day, confirming service, and knowing each county clerk's preferences cuts days off the administrative lag.
  • Warrant follow-through — landlords who don't track the warrant issuance or miss the officer's scheduling call can wait another 2–4 weeks for the next available date.
  • Make-ready pipeline — starting the vendor queue before the lockout date means the unit is listed within days, not weeks, of possession.

What Does the Full Timeline Look Like?

Every eviction is different, but a straightforward non-payment case in New Jersey — where the tenant does not contest, does not request adjournments, and does not pay to stay — typically runs:

  • Day 0–3: Notice to Quit period (non-payment track; attorney-review-pending)
  • Day 4–7: Complaint filed at Special Civil Part
  • Day 7–49 (approx.): Summons served, hearing date assigned (~5–6 weeks out; attorney-review-pending)
  • Hearing day: Judgment entered (if uncontested)
  • Post-judgment: Mandatory waiting period + warrant issuance (~1–2 weeks; attorney-review-pending)
  • Officer notice + lockout: Final 3 business days + execution

Realistic minimum: 7–10 weeks end-to-end for an uncontested non-payment case. Contested cases, adjournments, or "pay and stay" exercises can push the timeline to 4–6 months or more. Every week a unit sits occupied and non-paying is a week of lost rent — which is why starting the process correctly, immediately, matters.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does an NJ eviction take from start to finish?

An uncontested non-payment eviction in New Jersey typically takes 7–10 weeks from the date the Notice to Quit is served through the officer lockout, assuming no adjournments or "pay and stay" exercises. Contested cases or those involving lease violations (which require a 30-day notice) often run 3–6 months. These are estimates based on current court practice and are attorney-review-pending — actual timelines vary by county.

What is a 3-day Notice to Quit in New Jersey?

A 3-day Notice to Quit is the first formal step in a non-payment of rent eviction under NJSA 2A:18-53(b). It notifies the tenant that they owe rent, states the amount, and demands payment or vacatur within three days. If the tenant neither pays nor vacates, the landlord may then file a complaint at the Special Civil Part. The notice must be properly served and documented — a defective notice can result in case dismissal.

Can a tenant stop an eviction in New Jersey after a judgment is entered?

Yes — New Jersey law historically gives tenants the right to "pay and stay" by paying all outstanding rent and court costs after a judgment for possession but before the warrant for removal is executed. This right has limits (including a cap on how many times it can be exercised per year) and is subject to current legislative rules — confirm current parameters with a qualified NJ attorney. Tenants may also file certain motions to vacate a default judgment.

Who serves the eviction notice in New Jersey?

The landlord or property manager serves the Notice to Quit — typically via personal service (hand-delivery to the tenant or an adult household member) or certified mail with a certificate of mailing. After a court complaint is filed, the summons is served by a court officer, not the landlord. Proof of service for every notice must be documented and retained, as it becomes part of the court record.

What does a property manager do during the eviction process?

A professional property manager handles the landlord-side work at every step: preparing and serving legally accurate notices, filing the court complaint, assembling the evidence package (ledger, lease, certified mail logs), coordinating the court runner, attending the hearing, tracking warrant issuance, scheduling the officer lockout, and triggering the make-ready process immediately after possession. This reduces the risk of procedural errors that extend the timeline and keeps the owner informed without requiring them to appear in court.
