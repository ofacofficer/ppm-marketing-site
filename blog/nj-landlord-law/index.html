NJ Landlord Law & Compliance

New Jersey Landlord-Tenant Law:
The Complete Guide (2026)

By Dan Rivera · Updated April 2026

New Jersey has some of the most tenant-protective landlord-tenant laws in the country. This guide covers what every NJ property owner needs to know — security deposits, eviction rules, rent control, habitability, and more.

Why NJ landlord-tenant law is different

New Jersey is one of the most tenant-protective states in the nation. Unlike most states where landlord-tenant law is relatively straightforward, NJ layers state statutes with municipal ordinances — so the rules in Jersey City are materially different from the rules in Parsippany, even though both are in the same state.

Getting it wrong is expensive. Security deposit violations can cost you double the amount wrongfully withheld. An improper eviction notice resets the entire clock. Failure to register a property in a rent-controlled municipality can eliminate your ability to collect rent increases for years.

This pillar guide covers the key areas of NJ landlord-tenant law. Each section links to a detailed guide on that topic.

1. Security deposits in New Jersey

New Jersey security deposit law is detailed and specific. The key rules:

  • Maximum: 1.5x monthly rent (last month's rent collected upfront counts toward this limit)
  • Storage: Must be held in an interest-bearing account at a NJ bank, separate from your personal funds
  • Annual interest: Must be paid to the tenant each year — as cash or rent credit
  • Return timeline: 30 days after move-out (5 days for fire, flood, or condemnation)
  • Deductions: Itemized statement required; only unpaid rent and actual damage allowed
  • Penalty for violations: Double damages plus attorney's fees

2. Eviction law in New Jersey

New Jersey is a "just cause" eviction state — you cannot evict a tenant simply because a lease expired or because you want to sell. You must have a legally recognized ground for eviction, serve the proper written notice, and follow the court process exactly.

Common grounds for eviction include non-payment of rent, habitual late payment, lease violations, and certain criminal activity. The eviction process runs through NJ Superior Court, Special Civil Part.

Self-help evictions — changing locks, removing belongings, cutting utilities — are illegal in NJ and expose you to significant liability.

3. Rent control in New Jersey

There is no statewide rent control law in NJ. However, over a dozen municipalities have their own rent control ordinances — and each has different caps, exemptions, and administrative requirements.

Key rent-controlled municipalities include Jersey City, Hoboken, Newark, East Orange, Asbury Park, Fort Lee, and others. If your property is in one of these municipalities, you must understand the local rules before increasing rent or taking possession.

4. Habitability standards

NJ landlords must maintain rental properties in a livable, habitable condition. Key requirements include:

  • Working heat (minimum 68°F October 1 through May 1)
  • Hot and cold running water
  • Functional plumbing and electrical systems
  • Structurally sound building
  • Freedom from pest and rodent infestation
  • Working smoke and carbon monoxide detectors

Tenants who are denied habitable conditions have the right to withhold rent or make repairs and deduct the cost. Proactive maintenance prevents these situations from escalating.

5. Required disclosures

NJ landlords must provide specific disclosures before a tenant moves in:

  • Truth in Renting guide (required for all residential leases)
  • Lead paint disclosure (pre-1978 buildings)
  • Flood zone notification (if property is in a designated flood zone)
  • Where the security deposit is held (bank name and account)
  • Landlord identity and contact information
