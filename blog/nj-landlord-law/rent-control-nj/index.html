Rent Control in New Jersey: How Municipal Ordinances Work

By Proactive Property Management · Published July 2026
By Proactive Property Management · July 11, 2026 · Proactive Property Management

New Jersey has no statewide rent control — but that does not mean rent increases are unregulated. More than 100 of the state's 564 municipalities have adopted local rent control ordinances, each with its own cap, exemptions, and filing requirements. Whether your rental sits in one town or the next can change your allowable annual increase from unlimited to under 4 percent.

This guide explains how municipal rent control works, who is typically exempt, what registration involves, and what limits still apply in towns with no ordinance at all.

Rent control in NJ is municipal, not statewide

New Jersey leaves rent regulation to its municipalities, and each ordinance is a piece of local law: the cap, the buildings covered, the exemptions, and the board procedures all vary town by town. Jersey City, Newark, Elizabeth, and Edison each run different systems, and two adjacent towns can be night and day. The first step for any owner is confirming whether an ordinance applies — our free NJ Rent Control Lookup covers every municipality in the state. Because ordinances are amended regularly, verify the current text with your municipality before setting an increase.

How the caps typically work

Most ordinances use one of two structures:

  • CPI-based caps. The allowable increase tracks the change in the Consumer Price Index — sometimes the full CPI change, sometimes a fraction of it, and often with a fixed ceiling on top (for example, CPI up to a maximum of 4 percent).
  • Fixed-percentage caps. A flat annual limit, commonly in the 2 to 6 percent range.

Nearly all ordinances allow one increase per 12-month period. Many also permit add-ons above the base cap with board approval: property tax surcharges, capital improvement surcharges, and hardship increases when the capped rent will not cover operating costs.

Common exemptions

  • New construction. State law lets owners of newly constructed multiple dwellings claim an exemption from local rent control for up to 30 years, if properly filed (N.J.S.A. 2A:42-84.1 et seq.).
  • Owner-occupied small buildings. Many ordinances exempt owner-occupied two- to four-unit buildings.
  • Single-family homes and condos. Exempt in some towns, covered in others — never assume.
  • Vacancy decontrol. Where the ordinance allows it, rent can reset to market when a tenant voluntarily vacates, then becomes the new controlled base for the next tenancy. Some towns require filing a vacancy decontrol certificate.

Exemptions are ordinance-specific and often have paperwork attached. An exemption you never claimed in writing may not protect you.

Registration and rent leveling boards

Rent-controlled municipalities typically run a rent leveling board (sometimes called a rent control board) and require landlords to register their rents — often annually — so the town has a lawful base rent on file for every unit. Boards do three main jobs: they set and record base rents, they hear landlord applications for surcharges and hardship increases, and they hear tenant challenges claiming an increase exceeded the cap.

What happens if you do not register

Skipping registration is the expensive mistake. Depending on the ordinance, consequences include:

  • Rollback of the rent to the last lawful registered amount
  • Refunds or rent credits to the tenant for overcharges — which can reach back years
  • Municipal fines for each violation
  • Loss of the right to take otherwise-allowable increases until registration is brought current

An unlawful increase can also undermine a nonpayment case: if part of the rent you sue for was never legally chargeable, the case gets harder. See our guide to the NJ eviction process for why clean paperwork matters there.

Raising rent where there is no rent control

In towns without an ordinance there is no numeric cap, but two limits still apply.

The increase cannot be unconscionable. New Jersey courts can refuse to enforce an extreme increase in a nonpayment eviction. There is no bright-line percentage; judges weigh the size of the increase against market rents, the condition of the unit, and the landlord's costs. Large jumps are safer when documented against comparable local rents.

You must follow the procedure. Rent cannot change mid-lease unless the lease says so. For a month-to-month tenancy, an increase requires terminating the existing tenancy and offering a new one at the new rent: a written notice to quit plus an offer of the new terms, delivered at least one full calendar month before the increase takes effect. A notice served mid-month does not start the clock until the first of the next month.

Frequently asked questions

How do I find out if my town has rent control?

Start with the NJ Rent Control Lookup, which covers all 564 municipalities, then confirm the current ordinance text with the municipal clerk or rent leveling board — caps and exemptions change.

How much can I raise rent in a town without rent control?

There is no fixed cap, but the increase must not be unconscionable and must be delivered with proper notice — at least one full calendar month for month-to-month tenancies. Increases far above local market rates invite a court challenge.

Does rent control limit what I can charge a new tenant?

It depends on the ordinance. In towns with vacancy decontrol you can generally reset to market between tenants, and the new rent becomes the controlled base going forward. In towns without it, the cap follows the unit, not the tenant. Verify with your municipality before re-listing.
