NJ Eviction Process Timeline: A Step-by-Step Guide for Landlords (2026)

By Proactive Property Management · Published July 2026
By Proactive Property Management · July 18, 2026 · Proactive Property Management

Estimated read time: 8 minutes  |  Last reviewed: July 2026

Evicting a tenant in New Jersey is one of the most legally regulated actions a landlord can take. The state's Anti-Eviction Act [Attorney Review Required] protects tenants extensively — which means landlords who skip steps, serve the wrong notice, or attempt shortcuts can find their case dismissed and have to start over from zero.

This guide walks you through every stage of the lawful New Jersey eviction process so you know what to expect before you begin.

Overview: The Five-Stage NJ Eviction Sequence

Every lawful NJ eviction follows the same spine, in order:

  1. Valid legal grounds exist — you cannot evict without cause recognized under NJ law [Attorney Review Required].
  2. Written Notice to Quit — the tenant receives formal written notice before any court filing.
  3. Complaint filed in Special Civil Part — the landlord-tenant court at the county courthouse.
  4. Court hearing — a judge hears both sides and issues a judgment for possession (or not).
  5. Warrant for Removal and officer lock-out — only a court officer executes the physical removal.

Each stage has mandatory waiting periods. Skipping any stage is not a shortcut — it is a case-killer.

Approximate minimum timeline from first notice to lock-out, assuming no tenant defenses or court delays: several weeks to several months [Attorney Review Required]. Build that into your planning.

Step 1 — Establish Valid Grounds

New Jersey does not permit "no-cause" evictions in most residential situations [Attorney Review Required]. You must have a recognized legal cause before serving any notice. The most common grounds include:

  • Non-payment of rent — tenant has failed to pay rent when due.
  • Habitual late payment — a documented pattern of repeated late payments [Attorney Review Required].
  • Lease violation — tenant has materially breached a lease term after written notice [Attorney Review Required].
  • Disorderly conduct — behavior that disturbs neighbors or damages the property [Attorney Review Required].
  • Damage to property — willful or grossly negligent damage beyond normal wear and tear [Attorney Review Required].
  • Expiration of lease — available in limited circumstances for certain property types [Attorney Review Required].
  • Owner/family occupancy — specific rules and notice requirements apply [Attorney Review Required].

Documentation is everything. Before you serve a single notice, gather your lease, rent payment ledger, any prior written warnings, photographs, written communications, and any third-party records (police reports, neighbor complaints in writing). Courts expect organized, dated evidence. Cases built on memory alone routinely fail.

Step 2 — Serve the Required Written Notice

A proper written Notice to Quit is a prerequisite to filing in court — you generally cannot skip it [Attorney Review Required]. The notice must:

  • State the specific legal grounds for the eviction.
  • Give the tenant the notice period required by law for that ground [Attorney Review Required for applicable day counts].
  • Be served in a manner recognized by NJ court rules [Attorney Review Required].
  • Identify the property and the parties clearly.

For non-payment of rent, the notice typically gives the tenant an opportunity to pay in full and avoid eviction — if they pay, the case ordinarily stops there for that billing cycle [Attorney Review Required]. For other grounds, the cure right and timeline vary by the specific violation.

Common notice errors that kill cases:

  • Using the wrong notice type for the grounds alleged.
  • Counting days incorrectly (weekends and holidays may affect counts [Attorney Review Required]).
  • Serving the notice by an unacceptable method.
  • Failing to keep proof of service.

Keep a copy of every notice and your proof of service. Courts will ask for it.

Step 3 — File the Complaint in the Special Civil Part

If the tenant does not cure or vacate within the notice period, the landlord may file a Complaint for Possession in the Special Civil Part, Landlord-Tenant Section, of the Superior Court in the county where the property is located.

The filing package typically includes:

  • The complaint form, stating the grounds and the relief requested.
  • A copy of the lease (if written).
  • Proof that the Notice to Quit was properly served.
  • Any supporting documentation for the grounds alleged.
  • Filing fees [Attorney Review Required for current fee schedule].

Once filed, the court schedules a hearing date and the tenant is served with the complaint and hearing notice by the court or by the landlord (rules vary [Attorney Review Required]).

Tip: File in the correct county. Filing in the wrong county wastes time and money.

Step 4 — Attend the Court Hearing

On the hearing date, both parties appear before a judge or hearing officer in the Special Civil Part. Here is what to expect:

  • Bring everything. Lease, notices with proof of service, rent ledger, photographs, communications — organized chronologically.
  • Tenant defenses are allowed. Common defenses include payment, habitability issues, improper notice, retaliation, or discrimination. A well-prepared landlord addresses these proactively.
  • Possible outcomes:
    • Judgment for possession in the landlord's favor — the landlord may then request a warrant for removal after a mandatory waiting period [Attorney Review Required].
    • Judgment for the tenant — the case is dismissed; the landlord must correct the defect or start over.
    • Consent order or payment plan — the parties agree to a structured resolution, often with conditions.
    • Adjournment — the hearing is postponed, extending your timeline.

If you prevail, a judgment for possession does not immediately authorize physical removal. A warrant for removal must still be issued and a mandatory post-judgment waiting period must pass [Attorney Review Required].

Step 5 — Warrant for Removal and Physical Lock-Out

After a judgment for possession, the landlord requests a Warrant for Removal from the court. The warrant is served on the tenant, who then has a final mandatory waiting period before an officer may execute it [Attorney Review Required for current timeframes].

The physical lock-out is carried out exclusively by a court-authorized officer (typically a court officer or constable). The landlord does not — and may not — participate in the removal.

⚠️ Self-help eviction is illegal in New Jersey. Changing the locks, removing the tenant's belongings, shutting off utilities, removing doors or windows, or otherwise attempting to force a tenant out without a court-issued warrant and officer execution exposes the landlord to significant civil liability — including damages, attorney's fees, and potential criminal exposure [Attorney Review Required]. There are no exceptions. Do not do it.

Once the warrant is executed by the officer, possession is restored to the landlord. Any property left behind by the tenant is subject to separate rules on disposition [Attorney Review Required].

What Slows Cases Down (and How to Avoid It)

Most eviction delays are avoidable with preparation:

  • Wrong or defective notice — the single most common case-killer. Get the notice type and service method right from the start.
  • Missing documentation at the hearing — arrive with every document organized. Courts move fast; a landlord who can't produce a signed lease loses credibility immediately.
  • Tenant requests for adjournment — courts often grant them; build extra weeks into your timeline assumption.
  • Counterclaims for habitability — if there are open maintenance issues at the property, address them before filing. An active habitability defense can derail or delay a non-payment case.
  • Incorrect county or courtroom — file in the Special Civil Part in the county where the property sits.

After the Lock-Out: What Comes Next

Regaining possession is not the end of your obligations:

  • Security deposit accounting — NJ law sets strict deadlines and requirements for returning or itemizing the security deposit after a tenancy ends [Attorney Review Required].
  • Abandoned property — you may not simply discard items left behind. Specific procedures apply [Attorney Review Required].
  • Money judgment — a judgment for possession does not automatically include a money judgment for unpaid rent or damages. A separate claim or amended judgment may be required [Attorney Review Required].
  • Property condition — document the unit's condition thoroughly before re-renting: dated photographs, written inspection report.
  • Re-renting — all fair housing obligations apply to the re-leasing process. Screen applicants using lawful, consistent, documented criteria applied uniformly.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does the NJ eviction process take?

The total timeline depends on the grounds for eviction, whether the tenant contests the case, and court scheduling in your county. From first notice to a warrant for removal, the process commonly spans several weeks to several months [Attorney Review Required for specific timeframes]. Building buffer time into your planning is strongly advised.

Can a landlord evict a tenant in New Jersey without going to court?

No. New Jersey law requires a court judgment for possession before any removal can take place. A landlord who changes locks, removes belongings, shuts off utilities, or otherwise forces a tenant out without a court order is committing an illegal self-help eviction and may face significant legal liability. Only a court-authorized officer may execute a warrant for removal.

What notice is required before filing for eviction in New Jersey?

Written notice to the tenant is required before a landlord may file a complaint in the Special Civil Part. The required notice type and the number of days the tenant has to cure or vacate depend on the specific legal grounds for the eviction. [Attorney Review Required for notice periods and statutory requirements.]

What happens at an NJ landlord-tenant court hearing?

Both the landlord and tenant appear before a judge in the Special Civil Part. The landlord presents evidence of the legal grounds — lease, notices served, rent ledger, documentation of violations. The tenant may raise defenses. The judge issues a judgment: for possession in the landlord's favor, for the tenant, or with conditions such as a payment plan. If the landlord prevails, a warrant for removal may be issued after a mandatory waiting period. [Attorney Review Required for procedural details.]

Does New Jersey allow no-cause evictions?

In most residential situations, New Jersey's Anti-Eviction Act requires a landlord to have a recognized legal cause before initiating eviction proceedings. Limited exceptions may apply to certain property types and ownership situations. [Attorney Review Required for applicability to your specific property and lease.]
