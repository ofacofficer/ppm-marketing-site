Is Hiring a Property Manager Worth It? A Break-Even Analysis for NJ Landlords

By Proactive Property Management · Published July 2026
By Proactive Property Management · July 11, 2026 · Proactive Property Management

On a $2,500-a-month New Jersey rental, a typical management fee of 8% to 12% of monthly collected rent works out to $200 to $300 a month, or $2,400 to $3,600 a year. That is a real cost, and whether it is worth paying is not a matter of opinion — it is arithmetic.

The math has three inputs: the hours self-management actually takes, the cost of the mistakes it invites, and what a professional recovers in market-rate rent and shorter vacancy. This article works through each input, then runs a side-by-side example so you can plug in your own numbers.

What self-management actually costs in hours

Self-management is not free; you pay in time. Realistic estimates for a single NJ unit:

  • Listing and marketing: 4 to 6 hours per vacancy — photos, listing copy, syndicating to rental sites, and answering inquiries.
  • Showings: 6 to 10 hours per vacancy, including scheduling, travel, and the no-shows.
  • Screening: 3 to 5 hours per placement — applications, credit and eviction reports, income verification, and landlord reference calls.
  • Rent collection: 1 to 2 hours a month when payments arrive on time; noticeably more when they do not.
  • Maintenance coordination: 2 to 4 hours a month getting quotes, scheduling vendors, and following up on repairs.
  • Bookkeeping: 1 to 2 hours a month tracking income and expenses, plus several hours at tax time.

In steady state that is roughly 5 to 8 hours a month. A year with one turnover adds another 15 to 25 hours. Most self-managing landlords put in 90 to 120 hours per unit per year.

The hidden cost of mistakes

Hours are the visible cost. The larger risk is the mistakes that inexperience invites:

  • A bad tenant placement. A placement that ends in nonpayment can take several months from court filing to removal in New Jersey, plus turnover repairs afterward. On a $2,500 rental, that is easily $7,500 to $15,000 in lost rent and costs.
  • Vacancy weeks. Every empty week on a $2,500 rental costs about $577. Four extra weeks of vacancy costs about $2,300 — most of a year's management fee, gone before you have saved anything.
  • Security deposit violations. New Jersey caps deposits at 1.5 months' rent, requires the money to sit in a separate interest-bearing account with written notice to the tenant, and requires an itemized return within 30 days of move-out. Wrongful withholding exposes you to double damages, often plus court costs. The details are in our guide to NJ security deposit rules.
  • Missed municipal registration. Most NJ municipalities require landlords to register rental units. Skipping it can mean fines, and it can stall an eviction case until the registration is cured.

When self-managing makes sense

Plenty of landlords should keep managing their own property. Self-management tends to work when most of these are true:

  • You live within a short drive of the property.
  • You own one unit, so the hours stay contained.
  • You are handy enough to handle small repairs yourself.
  • Your schedule allows daytime showings and the occasional emergency call.
  • You are willing to keep current on NJ landlord-tenant law, registration deadlines, and local ordinances.

When a manager pays for itself

The break-even math shifts toward professional management in four common situations:

  • Out-of-state owners. Showings, inspections, and emergencies do not work from three time zones away, and paying local help ad hoc erodes the DIY savings quickly.
  • Multiple units. At two or three units, 5 to 8 hours a month each stops being a side task and starts being a part-time job.
  • Higher-rent units. Tenants paying $3,000 a month expect responsive, professional service, and pricing errors are costlier — renting $150 under market gives up $1,800 a year.
  • Rent-controlled municipalities. In cities like Jersey City, annual increases must be calculated under a CPI-based formula and filed correctly; getting it wrong can mean rollbacks and refunds.

A worked example: DIY vs. managed at $2,500 a month

Assume a unit that should rent for $2,500. Two conservative, common patterns: the self-managed unit is priced $100 under market, and it sits vacant two extra weeks at turnover because listing, showings, and screening move slower.

Questions to ask before you hire

If the math points toward management, a fee quote is only useful once you know what sits behind it:

  1. Is the fee charged on rent collected or rent due?
  2. What is the complete fee schedule in writing — leasing, renewal, setup, technology, vacancy? Our NJ property management cost guide breaks down typical ranges for each.
  3. Do you mark up maintenance invoices, or do I see the vendor's actual bill?
  4. What is your average days-to-lease, and where do listings run?
  5. Who handles municipal registration and rent control filings?
  6. What are the termination terms if the relationship is not working?

Frequently asked questions

How much time does self-managing one NJ rental take?

Plan on 5 to 8 hours a month in steady state, plus 15 to 25 hours in any year with a turnover — roughly 90 to 120 hours per unit per year.

What does a property manager typically charge in New Jersey?

Most NJ companies charge 8% to 12% of monthly collected rent, plus a one-time leasing fee when a tenant is placed. Ask whether the percentage applies to rent collected or rent due, and get the full fee schedule in writing.

Is a property manager worth it for a single unit?

Often, yes — if you live far from the property, cannot take calls during work hours, or own in a rent-controlled municipality. If you are local, handy, and have the time, self-managing one unit can be the better deal.
